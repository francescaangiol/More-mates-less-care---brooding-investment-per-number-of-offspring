# More-mates-less-care---brooding-investment-per-number-of-offspring
In species with multiple mating and parental care, greater mating success may result in a higher number of offspring. This increase can lead to greater costs for the caregiving parent through greater total care effort. Alternatively, as offspring numbers increase, parents may reduce the relative amount of care per offspring. Reductions in care may increase costs for both the other parent and the offspring. To identify if higher numbers of offspring translate into higher parental effort, we compared the duration and frequency of parental care behaviours provided by the nocturnal glassfrog Hyalinobatrachium valerioi while attending different numbers of clutches. These frogs exhibit a sequentially polyandrous mating system, with each male caring for numerous clutches simultaneously. We recorded 37 males caring for 1–5 concurrent clutches using infrared cameras at night over two mating seasons. We found that as clutch quantity increased, time spent brooding per clutch decreased. However, total time brooding per night remained consistent across males. Additionally, clutches in late developmental stages received less care than clutches in early stages. These findings suggest that males adjust brooding behaviour to offspring needs without compromising their energetic investment with an increasing number of clutches and this could translate in costs to the offspring when males mate more.
